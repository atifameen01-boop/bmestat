Probability vs Non-Probability

Probability sampling guarantees every person has a known, non-zero probability of selection, allowing valid mathematical confidence intervals. Non-probability relies on researcher convenience or selection criteria, useful for exploratory/qualitative efforts.

Homogeneity vs Heterogeneity

If population values of interest are nearly identical (homogeneous), you need a very small sample size. Highly diverse populations (heterogeneous) require Stratified selection or larger overall samples to ensure accurate sub-group mapping.

Parametric vs Non-Parametric

Parametric tests (e.g., t-test, ANOVA) assume continuous data is normally distributed with homogeneous variance. If these assumptions are violated, you must switch to non-parametric equivalents (e.g., Mann-Whitney, Kruskal-Wallis) which analyze ranks instead of raw metrics.

Independent vs Paired (Repeated Measures)

Independent designs compare unrelated cohorts (e.g., Treatment Group vs Control Group). Paired designs track dependent units (e.g., Pre-test vs Post-test scores within the same patient pool), removing background demographic noise.

Based on standard degrees of freedom principles. It holds that the optimal error degrees of freedom ($E$) lies between 10 and 20. When $E < 10$, you increase Type II error rates; when $E > 20$, you violate the ethical Reduce principle of the 3Rs.
